1. The Problem & User Story

Sarah walks her golden retriever along Prospect Park lake every morning. Last summer, a toxic algal bloom went undetected for days — her dog got sick after drinking from the shore. Official water quality reports exist, but they're buried in government portals, updated infrequently, and impossible to act on.

DeepBlue is a community-driven water safety platform that fuses official sensor data (USGS) with real-time crowdsourced reports to produce a live Safety Forecast for local water bodies. Users open an interactive map, see color-coded safety markers, tap into any water body for an AI-generated advisory ("Safe for swimming, avoid pet contact near south shore"), and submit their own geo-tagged photo reports from the field.

3. System Architecture & Features

3.1 Data Ingestion Pipeline

  1. Official Stream: A scheduled Go routine fetches JSON payloads from the USGS Water Data API on a 15-minute interval. Parsed metrics (pH, temperature, turbidity) are upserted into the water_entities collection.

  2. Community Stream: Users upload geo-tagged photos via the frontend. Images are stored in AWS S3. A document is inserted into the community_reports collection with the S3 URL and location.

3.2 Change Stream Pipeline (MongoDB)

A MongoDB Change Stream watches the community_reports collection for new inserts. On each new report:

  1. The Go backend sends the image to Bedrock (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) as a multimodal prompt: "Analyze this water body photo. Identify any visual indicators of algal blooms, discoloration, foam, debris, or pollution. Return a risk label and confidence score."
  2. The returned risk labels are written back to the report document.
  3. The parent water_entity safety score is recalculated.

This demonstrates event-driven architecture with MongoDB as the reactive backbone — a strong signal for the MongoDB track.

3.3 The AI Reasoning Layer (RAG)

When a user taps on a water body for its advisory:

  • Retrieval: MongoDB Atlas Vector Search queries a knowledge_chunks collection — pre-embedded excerpts from EPA/WHO water safety guidelines.
  • Augmentation: The retrieved chunks are combined with the entity's live sensor data and recent community risk labels.
  • Generation: Bedrock (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) synthesizes everything into a human-readable safety advisory.

Why three collections? This avoids the unbounded embedded-array anti-pattern, enables independent indexing strategies, and lets us demonstrate MongoDB Aggregation Pipelines with $lookup to join entities with their reports — a much stronger showing for the MongoDB track.

5. Safety Index Algorithm

The Safety Index (S) is computed by the Go backend whenever sensor data or community reports change. It determines the marker color on the map:

S = (W_ph × ΔpH) + (W_turb × T_norm) + (W_vis × V_avg)
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                             3

Where:

  • ΔpH: Absolute deviation from neutral (7.0), normalized to 0–1.
  • T_norm: Turbidity in NTU, normalized against EPA thresholds (0–1).
  • V_avg: Average visual risk confidence from the last 5 community reports (0–1).
  • W: Weighting factors — default W_ph=0.3, W_turb=0.3, W_vis=0.4 (community signal weighted highest to reward engagement).

7. Pre-Seed Data Strategy

Do NOT rely on live API calls during the demo. Before the hackathon demo window:

  1. Pre-load 20–30 real water bodies (NYC/NJ area) into water_entities with actual USGS historical data.
  2. Seed 5–10 synthetic community reports per entity with realistic images and AI-generated risk labels.
  3. Pre-embed 50+ EPA/WHO guideline chunks into knowledge_chunks with their vector embeddings.
  4. Compute safety scores for all entities so the map looks populated and alive on first load.

Live ingestion runs in parallel but the demo never depends on it.

9. MongoDB Track Narrative

For the "Best Use of MongoDB" pitch, we demonstrate five distinct MongoDB capabilities in a single coherent application:

  1. Geospatial Indexing (2dsphere) — Powering the map: find all water entities and reports within a bounding box, proximity queries for nearby reports.
  2. Atlas Vector Search — RAG retrieval: semantic search over embedded EPA/WHO safety guidelines to provide context-aware advisories.
  3. Change Streams — Event-driven architecture: new community report → triggers AI analysis → updates safety score, all reactive with no polling.
  4. Aggregation Pipelines ($lookup, $geoNear) — Joining entities with their reports, computing rolling averages of community risk scores, filtering by recency and proximity.
  5. Atlas (Cloud-Native) — Fully managed cluster with monitoring, scaling, and integrated search — no self-hosted infrastructure.
